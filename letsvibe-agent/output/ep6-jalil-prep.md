## Guest Bio

Jalil Sebastian Wahdatehagh is the developer behind Visualize Value — Jack Butcher's technical right hand. Based south of Munich, he builds the smart contracts, onchain renderers, and software/hardware systems behind every major VV project: Checks, Opepen (87K+ ETH volume), Self Checkout (Art Basel Miami), Gas Wars (Art Blocks), and the open-source MINT protocol.

His path was not linear. German, dropped out of university twice, married young, started a family, attempted four startups — all failed. His fourth venture collapsed in 2020 after a 9-year partnership dissolved. He nearly quit entrepreneurship entirely. Then he found Jack Butcher's courses, went solo, and built his way into the VV orbit without asking permission. He calls himself a "gardener" and his first project was literally called "The Permissionless Apprentice."

He runs 1001.digital UG from Germany. His GitHub has 61 repos and 648 stars. He's appeared on the Vaulted (PROOF) podcast with Jack, Mastermind.fm, and How You Create.

## Three Conversation Threads

### 1. The Permissionless Apprentice (15 min)

The origin story. Four failed startups by 30. Finding VV. Building PunkScape as a portfolio piece. The moment Jack noticed.

**Questions:**
- You call yourself a "gardener" — what does that mean in the context of building software?
- You had four startups fail before this. What was different about the VV moment?
- "Permissionless Apprentice" — you literally named your first project that. What does permissionless apprenticeship look like in practice?
- When did the collaboration with Jack shift from "fan who builds things" to "creative partner"?

**Listen for:** The emotional weight of the 2020 failure. The "truly, thank you client" moment that pushed him into web3. The line between apprenticeship and partnership.

---

### 2. Jack Is Vibecoding Now (15 min)

Jack wrote in January 2026 ("Vibe Meta" on Substack): "I've been down the Claude Code rabbit hole the last couple of weeks, transitioning from technically illiterate to technically illiterate but with an infinitely patient teacher." He built Pattern (pattern.vv.xyz) — a prompt-sharing feed — as his first shipped vibecoded product.

Meanwhile Jalil has been writing smart contracts from scratch for years. What happens to the designer-developer partnership when the designer can suddenly ship code?

**Questions:**
- Jack is vibecoding with Claude Code now. How does that change your dynamic?
- Does it make you more important or less important when the non-technical partner can ship?
- What can Jack build now that he couldn't before? What still needs you?
- Pattern was Jack's first vibecoded product. Did you look at the code? What did you think?
- Is there a version of VV where Jack doesn't need a developer anymore?

**Listen for:** Honesty about whether this is threatening or liberating. The distinction between "can ship something" and "can build infrastructure." Where the human craft still matters.

---

### 3. Onchain Art as Physical Practice (15 min)

VV isn't just JPEGs. Self Checkout at Art Basel Miami was a pay-what-you-want kiosk — Jalil built both the software and the hardware. Checks Elements mapped onchain SVGs to lithographic prints on a vintage Mailander 202 press at Christie's. The MINT protocol is open-source infrastructure for permissionless creation.

**Questions:**
- Self Checkout made $114K from a pay-what-you-want kiosk at Art Basel. Walk me through building that — you did the software AND the hardware?
- Checks Elements — onchain SVG to physical monoprint on a vintage press. How do you bridge digital and physical?
- The Infinity Checks smart contract had a bug that got exploited. You and Jack took full responsibility and refunded everyone. What was that moment like?
- The MINT protocol is open source. Why give away infrastructure?
- You wrote on your blog about digital inheritance and civilization. What does it mean to build things that outlast you onchain?

**Listen for:** The craft of building physical installations. The vulnerability of the exploit story. Open source philosophy ("open source will always be more efficient than anything created with different incentives").
